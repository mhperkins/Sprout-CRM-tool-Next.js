## Purpose

A CRM is only as useful as the intelligence behind each record. A contact with just a name and email is a placeholder. A contact with verified role, mapped priorities, assessed relationship potential, and a clear cultivation path is an asset.

This protocol exists to ensure that every record imported into CRMManager.jsx is built on real research — not assumptions, not stale info, not fabricated details. The output of this protocol is a complete JSON profile ready to paste into the Import modal.

**Every new contact or organization gets a Research Brief before a first outreach is drafted.**

---

## Applies To

This protocol is adapted by contact type. The same phases run in every case, but the research questions shift depending on whether the subject is a:

- **Funder** — foundation, government agency, corporate giving program, or major donor
- **Partner** — nonprofit, social enterprise, or civic org for collaboration or referral
- **Community Builder** — event host, group facilitator, or platform user
- **Individual Supporter** — donor, volunteer, or champion of Sprout Society

Contact-type-specific guidance is called out in each phase.

**Discovery channels covered by this protocol:**
- Web and organizational research (all contact types)
- Instagram (primary discovery channel — see dedicated section below)
- ProPublica / 990 research (funders)
- Network and referral (all contact types)

## Tiering Reminder

| Tier | Meaning | Contact Cadence |
|---|---|---|
| A | Actively aligned, high value — pursue now | Weekly |
| B | Good fit, needs cultivation — maintain presence | Monthly |
| C | Long-term pipeline — track, don't push | Quarterly |

Tier assignment happens at the end of Phase D. Do not pre-assign tier before research is complete — early assumptions about tier often prove wrong once alignment is actually assessed.

## Phase A — Identity Verification
*Who are they, actually, right now?*

**Goal:** Establish verified, current facts about this contact or organization before anything else. Titles change. Orgs restructure. Emails go stale. This phase exists so the record you import is accurate on day one.

**Steps:**

1. Search the contact's full name + organization. Verify their current title and role via the org's website — not a third-party bio or a cached LinkedIn result.
2. If an organization record: confirm current legal name, website, primary address, and mission language from the live website — not from memory or secondary sources.
3. Check for any recent news: leadership changes, program pivots, new initiatives, mergers, closures. A 2-minute news search now can prevent an embarrassing outreach later.
4. For funders: verify current program areas and geographic focus. Do not assume last year's guidelines still apply.
5. For individuals: verify they still hold the role you found. People move. A LinkedIn or staff page check is the minimum.
6. Note the source and date for every verified fact. Flag anything you couldn't confirm as MEDIUM confidence.

**Never import an email address you generated from a name pattern.** Find it via the org's website, a verified press mention, or ask the user to provide it directly.

**Output:** Verified identity block — name, current title, current org, confirmed email (if found), confidence level on each field.

---

## Phase B — Mission and Priority Mapping
*What do they actually care about, and why does it overlap with us?*

**Goal:** Understand the contact or organization's current priorities deeply enough to explain — in one sentence — why Sprout Society is relevant to them. Generic mission overlap ("we both care about mental health") is not enough. This phase finds the specific, current, genuine connection.

**Steps:**

1. Read their website fully — not just the homepage. Programs pages, About pages, recent blog posts or news. Note the language they choose, not what you'd assume.
2. Search for any public statements, press coverage, or interviews from the last 12–18 months. What problems are they describing? What solutions are they advancing?
3. **For contacts discovered via Instagram:** Read their last 12–20 posts before their website. Instagram content is often more current and candid than website copy — it reveals what they're actively thinking about, not just their evergreen positioning. Note recurring themes, language patterns, and what they amplify from others.
4. **For funders:** What is their current theory of change? What do they believe causes the problem they fund? How does Sprout Society's model fit or challenge that belief?
4. **For partners:** What populations do they serve? What gaps or referral needs do they have that Sprout Society could fill? What might they need from us — co-programming, space, calendar visibility, participant pathways?
5. **For community builders:** What type of programming are they running or trying to run? What platform or infrastructure gaps do they have? What draws them to community-building work?
6. **For individual supporters:** What causes or organizations have they publicly supported? What values or experiences drive their giving or volunteering? (LinkedIn, social media, press, event attendee lists — public signals only.)
7. Answer in writing: *What is the most specific, honest reason this contact should care about Sprout Society?* If you can't write that sentence, keep researching.

**Output:** Priority map — their current stated priorities, the specific overlap with Sprout Society, and the one-sentence connection statement.

---

## Phase C — Relationship History
*Have we met? What do they already know about us?*

**Goal:** Surface any existing connection between this contact and Sprout Society before any outreach is planned. A "cold" contact who attended one of our events is warm. A "new" funder who previously declined us is not new — and outreach that ignores the prior interaction will land badly.

**Steps:**

1. Check the Sprout Society outreach log (in CRMManager if the tool is running, or ask the user). Has anyone from this org or individual ever been contacted before?
2. Check the Grant Manager project for any funder overlaps — has this org appeared in any grant research, LOI, or submission?
3. Check event records, community portal sign-ups, or calendar listings for any trace of this person or org in the Sprout ecosystem.
4. **Check Instagram engagement history:** Has this account followed @sproutsocietyorg? Have they liked, commented on, or shared any Sprout content? Has Sprout ever engaged with their posts? Even a single meaningful comment exchange counts as a touchpoint — log it. If Sprout has never appeared on their radar, that confirms cold status and shapes the opening move.
5. Ask the user directly: "Do you know this person? Have you met them at an event, a convening, or through another funder?" This takes 10 seconds and often surfaces the most important context.
5. If there is prior history: document it as a touchpoint, note the outcome, and adjust relationship status accordingly.
6. If there is no prior history: confirm "cold" status and note it. Do not assume warm.

**Output:** Relationship history note — prior interactions documented as touchpoints, relationship status set (cold / warm / active / lapsed), any warm connection identified.

---

## Phase D — Alignment Assessment and Tier Assignment
*An honest read before we invest time.*

**Goal:** Make a clear-eyed determination of how well this contact or org fits Sprout Society's needs right now, and what tier of cultivation investment is appropriate. This is where you save time by not over-investing in low-fit relationships.

**Steps:**

1. List Sprout Society's strongest alignment points with this contact — be specific. "They fund mental health" is not specific. "They explicitly fund peer-led, non-clinical community programs in Brooklyn" is specific.
2. List any weak points or flags: geographic mismatch, program model misalignment, size/budget gap, stated priorities that don't match our work, cultural or political misalignment.
3. **For funders:** Is Sprout Society eligible? Check budget thresholds, org age, geographic restrictions, and program type eligibility explicitly.
4. **For partners:** Is there a clear mutual benefit? What does each side gain? Is there a referral pathway, co-programming opportunity, or shared constituency?
5. **For community builders:** Are they a good fit for the platform? Do their events or groups serve the population we're trying to reach? Are they currently active?
6. Assign tier with a one-sentence justification. Tier A requires active alignment *right now*, not potential future alignment.
7. Note: if fit is genuinely weak, say so. A C-tier contact with no near-term activation path may not be worth importing at all — flag this and let the user decide.

**Output:** Alignment assessment — strengths, flags, tier assignment with justification, import recommendation (proceed / hold / skip).

---

## Phase E — Cultivation Path
*What happens next, in what order, over what timeline?*

**Goal:** Define a specific, realistic first step and a rough 3-step cultivation arc before the record is imported. A contact with no cultivation path is a dead record — it will sit in the CRM with a "next_action" field that never gets filled.

**Steps:**

1. Define the first touchpoint: What is the single best way to make contact or deepen the relationship? Email? Event invite? Intro through a mutual connection? Request a call? **For Instagram-sourced contacts: is the right first move a comment, a DM, a story reply, or a follow + engage sequence?** This must be specific — "reach out" is not a plan.
2. Identify the right timing: Is there a deadline, event, cycle, or moment that makes outreach particularly well-timed right now? (Funding cycle opening, upcoming convening, recent news hook, seasonal programming moment. **For Instagram contacts:** a timely reply to a recent post or story is often more natural and higher-converting than a cold DM.)
3. Sketch a 3-step arc:
   - **Step 1:** First touch — goal is to open the relationship or re-engage it.
   - **Step 2:** Cultivation — a second interaction that adds value and deepens the connection. This could be sharing relevant resources, inviting to an event, making an introduction, or following up on a conversation.
   - **Step 3:** Ask or activation — the specific thing Sprout Society wants from this relationship: a grant application, a partnership MOU, a platform listing, a donation conversation, a volunteer commitment.
4. Note any dependencies: "Can't move to Step 2 until we hear back from Step 1." "Step 3 depends on having a confirmed event date." These dependencies belong in the `next_action` notes field.
5. Set a realistic next_action_date. If you don't know, default to within 30 days for A-tier, 60 days for B-tier, 90 days for C-tier.

**Output:** Cultivation path — first touchpoint with timing, 3-step arc, next_action and next_action_date ready for the JSON profile.

---

## Phase F — Network Intelligence
*Who else do we know who knows them?*

**Goal:** Identify any mutual connections, shared networks, or ecosystem overlaps that could warm this relationship before first contact. A warm introduction from a trusted third party is almost always better than a cold email.

**Steps:**

1. Ask the user: "Does anyone at Sprout Society know this person or org?" This includes staff, board, volunteers, advisors, and active community builders on the platform.
2. Search for shared networks: Are they a grantee of a funder Sprout Society also works with? Do they show up on the same coalition, convening, or mailing list? Have they appeared at events in the same ecosystem?
3. Check whether any current Sprout Society partners or community builders have a relationship with this contact — a warm intro from a peer org is often more effective than one from the nonprofit itself.
4. If a warm connection exists: document it, note who holds the connection, and factor it into the cultivation path (Step 1 becomes "ask [name] for an introduction" rather than direct cold outreach).
5. If no connection exists: confirm cold status and proceed with a well-researched cold outreach strategy. The absence of a warm connection is not a blocker — it just changes the approach.

**Output:** Network intelligence note — warm connections identified or confirmed absent, any intro paths documented.

---

## Phase G — Profile Compilation and JSON Generation
*Turn research into an importable record.*

**Goal:** Compile everything from Phases A–F into a complete JSON profile matching the CRM schema, ready to paste into the CRMManager Import modal.

**Steps:**

1. Fill every field in the schema — never leave a field blank without a reason. If a field is genuinely unknown, use `""` and flag it in `notes` as a research gap to resolve.
2. Set `confidence` field accurately:
   - **HIGH:** Field verified via primary source (live website, official 990, confirmed contact)
   - **MEDIUM:** Field sourced from secondary source, press mention, or may be dated — flag for verification
3. Populate `touchpoints` with any prior interactions surfaced in Phase C. If no prior interactions, leave as `[]`.
4. Write `next_action` as a complete, actionable sentence — not a fragment. Bad: "Email." Good: "Send intro email referencing their current capacity-building cohort and proposing a 20-minute call."
5. For funder contacts: populate `linked_grants` with any active or past grant IDs from the Grant Manager project.
6. For org records: set `primary_contact_id` if an individual record is being generated in the same session.
7. Review the full JSON before delivering. Check for fabricated fields, missing required values, and consistency between org and individual records if both are being generated.

**Deliver org record and individual record(s) as separate JSON objects**, clearly labeled. If both are ready to import in the same session, deliver them together with a note about import order (org first, then individuals linked to it).

**Output:** Complete, importable JSON profile(s) — organization record, individual record(s), all fields populated, confidence flags set, next action defined.

---

## Sequencing Note

These phases are designed to run in order, but research rarely surfaces in a clean sequence. Practical guidance:

- **Phase A (Identity) must come first.** Everything else builds on verified facts.
- **Phase C (Relationship History) should happen before Phase E (Cultivation Path).** You can't plan first contact without knowing if contact has already happened.
- **Phase D (Alignment Assessment) should not be finalized until Phases B and C are complete.** Alignment depends on both their priorities and our history with them.
- **Phase G (JSON) is always last.** It compiles everything — don't start filling the schema until you have the research.
- **Phases F (Network Intelligence) can run in parallel** with Phases B–D — it's a quick user question, not a research deep-dive.

## Instagram Relationship-Building Protocol
*The primary discovery and cultivation channel for Sprout Society right now.*

### Why Instagram Gets Its Own Section

Most of Sprout Society's relationship pipeline — community builders, local partner orgs, individual supporters, and even some funders — will first surface on Instagram before they ever appear in an email thread or a grant database. The way relationships are built on Instagram is categorically different from the way they're built via email: it's public, it's slow, it rewards consistency over intensity, and it works best when it doesn't feel like outreach at all.

This section governs how Instagram-sourced contacts move from "account we noticed" to "record in the CRM."

---

### Stage 1 — Discovery
*Finding accounts worth tracking before committing to cultivation.*

Instagram discovery happens in three ways for Sprout Society:

**Inbound discovery** — accounts that find Sprout first. Followers, commenters, story reactors, accounts that tag or mention Sprout. These are the warmest leads and should be checked weekly.

**Hashtag and location discovery** — searching hashtags and geotags relevant to Sprout's work. Priority searches:
- `#brooklyncommunity` `#brooklynevents` `#brooklynnonprofit`
- `#mentalhealthcommunity` `#peersupport` `#loneliness` `#socialconnection`
- `#thirdplace` `#communitybuilding` `#nycnonprofit`
- Location tags: Bushwick, Ridgewood, Williamsburg, Bed-Stuy, Crown Heights, Prospect Heights
- Adjacent org tags: accounts of orgs Sprout already knows and trusts — who are *they* following and engaging with?

**Ecosystem discovery** — looking at who follows, comments on, or is tagged by accounts already in the CRM. The best community builders often cluster: if you find one, their network contains more.

**Discovery threshold:** Not every interesting account becomes a CRM record. Before creating a profile, ask: Is this account actively posting (within the last 60 days)? Does their content suggest genuine alignment — not just aesthetic overlap? Is there a plausible path to a real relationship? If yes to all three, proceed to Stage 2.

---

### Stage 2 — Account Research
*What does this account actually tell us?*

Before creating a CRM record for an Instagram-sourced contact, run a focused account audit. Claude cannot access Instagram directly — the user provides the account handle and relevant content. Claude's job is to help analyze and interpret what the user shares.

**Steps:**

1. **Handle and identity:** Confirm their handle, display name, bio, and any linked website or email. Check if the bio links to an org, a Linktree, or a personal site — follow those links and run Phase A (Identity Verification) on what you find.

2. **Content audit (last 12–20 posts):** What is this account actually posting about? Look for:
   - Recurring themes and topics
   - The specific language they use — what words do they choose for the work they do?
   - Who they tag, amplify, or collaborate with
   - What they respond to in comments
   - Whether they post about Brooklyn / NYC specifically
   - Any mentions of Sprout Society or adjacent orgs

3. **Audience signals:** Follower count matters less than engagement quality. A 400-follower account with genuine community in the comments is more valuable than a 10K account with no replies. Note whether comments feel like a real community or a passive audience.

4. **Posting cadence:** Are they actively posting? A dormant account (60+ days since last post) is a low-priority prospect regardless of content quality. Note cadence in the profile.

5. **Contact path:** Is there an email in the bio or linked site? A DM-open signal? This determines what the cultivation path looks like.

6. **Assign a preliminary contact type and tier** based on account audit findings. This may be revised after web research (Phase A/B) confirms or contradicts what Instagram suggests.

---

### Stage 3 — Engagement Sequence
*How to build the relationship before making any ask.*

The Instagram cultivation arc has a different shape than email cultivation. It is slower and more ambient. The goal of Stages 1–3 is to become a recognized, valued presence in their feed *before* any direct contact is made. This is not manipulation — it's how relationships actually form on the platform.

**The engagement sequence for a new Instagram contact:**

**Week 1–2: Follow and observe**
- Follow the account from @sproutsocietyorg
- Do not comment or DM yet
- Read their last 20 posts to build genuine familiarity
- Note the post or story that is most resonant — this becomes the opening for first contact

**Week 2–4: Meaningful engagement (public)**
- Leave 2–3 substantive comments across different posts over this period
- Comments must be genuinely specific — not "Love this!" but something that demonstrates real attention to what they posted. Reference the actual content.
- Share or save a post if it's genuinely useful to Sprout's community
- If they post a story, respond to it — story replies feel personal even when they're not

**Week 4–6: First direct contact (if appropriate)**
- Only move to DM after public engagement has been established and ideally reciprocated in some way (a like on a comment, a follow-back, a reply)
- The first DM is not a pitch. It is an extension of the conversation already happening. Start from the most recent genuine exchange.
- Keep it short. Two to three sentences: who you are (briefly), what you noticed about their work, and one low-ask opening. Examples:
  - "We've been following your Sunday sessions and they look so needed — would love to know more about what's bringing people in."
  - "We're building a community calendar for Brooklyn mental health and wellness orgs and we'd love to include your events if you'd want that."
  - "Your post about third spaces really landed — we're working on the same thing from a different angle. Would love to connect sometime."
- Do not send a mission paragraph. Do not ask for anything that requires effort to fulfill. Do not attach a deck.

**Week 6+: Cultivation and activation**
- If they respond: continue the conversation, look for a natural next step (event invite, intro call, calendar listing, collaboration)
- If they don't respond to the first DM: wait 3–4 weeks, continue light engagement, try one more DM with a different hook — usually an event invite or something concrete and low-stakes
- If they don't respond to a second contact: set status to "warm" (not cold — the engagement happened), set next_action to "revisit in 3 months," and move on

---

### Stage 4 — CRM Import
*When does an Instagram contact become a CRM record?*

**Create the CRM record at Stage 2 (account research), not at Stage 3.** The record is how you track where you are in the sequence. If you wait until Stage 3 to create it, you lose the engagement history.

**Instagram-specific fields to populate in the JSON:**

In the `notes` field, always include:
```
Instagram: @[handle] | Followers: [approx] | Posting cadence: [active/intermittent/dormant] | Engagement quality: [high/medium/low]
```

In `tags`, include relevant discovery tags:
```
["instagram_sourced", "brooklyn", "community_builder"] 
// or "partner", "individual_supporter" etc.
```

In `touchpoints`, log each engagement stage as it happens:
```json
{
  "date": "2026-04-25",
  "type": "social",
  "direction": "outbound",
  "summary": "Followed @handle from @sproutsocietyorg. Left comment on April 20 post about Sunday community dinners.",
  "outcome": "Comment received 2 likes including from @handle",
  "next_step": "Continue engagement — one more comment this week, then reassess DM timing"
}
```

In `next_action`, be specific about the stage:
- "Leave second substantive comment on @handle — respond to their post about [topic]"
- "Send first DM to @handle referencing [specific post] — keep to 2–3 sentences, no ask"
- "Follow up DM to @handle with event invite to [event name] on [date]"

### Tracking Instagram Engagement in the CRM

Every meaningful Instagram interaction is a touchpoint. Log them as type `social`. Use these conventions:

| Interaction | Direction | Summary convention |
|---|---|---|
| We followed their account | outbound | "Followed @handle from @sproutsocietyorg" |
| We commented on their post | outbound | "Commented on [date] post re: [topic]" |
| They followed us back | inbound | "@handle followed @sproutsocietyorg" |
| They liked/replied to our comment | inbound | "@handle replied to our comment on [post]" |
| We sent a DM | outbound | "Sent first DM re: [topic/hook]. No ask — opened conversation." |
| They replied to a DM | inbound | "@handle replied to DM — [summary of what they said]" |
| Story reply | outbound | "Replied to @handle story re: [content]" |

If a relationship moves off Instagram (they email us, we get on a call, they attend an event), log that as a separate touchpoint with the appropriate type and note the channel shift.
